NaturalReader is a good product. It's been around since 2003, it helped thousands of people with dyslexia, and for a long time it was one of the best free text-to-speech tools on the web.

But things have changed. If you've searched for a **NaturalReader alternative**, you're probably not the only one. Over the past year or two, NaturalReader started requiring an account just to use the basic features. The free tier got more restrictive. The interface got more complicated. And the price — $99.50/year — is harder to justify when you just want to listen to articles or documents.

So we went through the five most-searched alternatives, tested them honestly, and laid out exactly who each one is right for.

## What to Look For in a NaturalReader Alternative

Before the list, here's what actually matters when you're comparing text-to-speech tools:

**Natural-sounding voices.** The old robotic TTS voices made it hard to focus. Modern AI voices from Google, ElevenLabs, and others sound close to human. This is the single biggest factor in whether you'll actually use the tool.

**Word-by-word highlighting.** This is a game-changer for reading comprehension, ADHD, and dyslexia. The word lights up as it's read aloud so your eyes know where to look. NaturalReader doesn't do this in its web version — which is surprising given their accessibility focus. If this matters to you, read more about [text to speech with word highlighting](/blog/text-to-speech-word-highlighting).

**URL-to-audio.** The ability to paste a link and have the tool extract the article text automatically. Without this, you have to manually copy and paste every article you want to listen to. That gets old fast.

**PDF and file import.** Most people who use TTS seriously are listening to PDFs — research papers, textbooks, reports. Make sure the tool supports this without an extra hoop.

**Price.** A lot of TTS tools are priced for enterprise or power users. If you're a student or casual reader, you shouldn't need to spend $100+/year.

**No-login option.** Some people don't want to create yet another account just to try a product. A tool that lets you preview the voice quality before signing up earns real trust.

## 2. Speechify — Most Powerful, But Expensive

**Price:** $139/year ($11.58/month)

**Best for:** Power users who want every feature and don't mind paying for it.

Speechify is the market leader in text-to-speech. It has 200+ voices, a polished iOS and Android app, a Chrome extension that reads anything in your browser, OCR for printed text, and celebrity voice options. It does word highlighting. It has URL reading. It's genuinely impressive.

The problem is the price. $139/year is expensive for what most people actually need, which is: listen to articles and documents with a natural voice. For that use case, you're paying a lot of extra money for features you might never touch.

Speechify also requires an account from the start — no preview without signing up. And like NaturalReader, the free tier is very limited.

If you're already evaluating [Speechify alternatives](/blog/speechify-alternatives), the calculus is similar: ReadTo delivers the core features at a fraction of the price.

**When Speechify makes sense:** You want celebrity voices, you rely heavily on the iOS app, you use OCR regularly, or your employer is covering the cost.

## 3. Microsoft Immersive Reader — Free, But Only in Microsoft Apps

**Price:** Free

**Best for:** Students and educators already using Microsoft 365.

Microsoft Immersive Reader is a reading support tool built into Word, OneNote, Teams, Outlook, and several other Microsoft apps. It reads text aloud with word highlighting, supports multiple languages, and has a clean, distraction-free interface. For a free tool, it's genuinely well-designed.

The hard limit: it only works inside Microsoft's ecosystem. You can't use it to read a random article, a PDF from outside OneDrive, or anything outside a Microsoft app. If you want to listen to a web article, you have to copy it into Word first — which is its own form of friction.

The voices are decent but not at the same level as Google or ElevenLabs neural TTS. They vary by language and platform.

**When Immersive Reader makes sense:** You're a student or teacher already working in Microsoft 365 every day, and most of what you want to read is already in Word or OneNote.

## 4. ElevenLabs — Best Voices, Wrong Tool for Reading

**Price:** Free tier (10,000 characters/month) · $5/month (Starter) · $22/month (Creator)

**Best for:** Voice cloning, audio production, and developers building TTS into apps.

ElevenLabs has the most realistic AI voices available right now. If you've heard an AI voice and thought "wait, that might actually be human," there's a good chance it was ElevenLabs.

The problem is that ElevenLabs is built for generating audio files, not for reading. There's no document reader, no PDF import, no URL extraction, no word highlighting. You paste text, it generates an audio file, and you download it. That's the core product.

For casual reading — listening to articles, documents, or ebooks — it's the wrong tool. The character limits on free and starter tiers are also low for reading use cases. A typical article is 1,500–2,000 words, which is about 9,000–12,000 characters. The free tier runs out fast.

Where ElevenLabs genuinely shines: creating voiceovers, cloning your own voice, building audio content for podcasts or videos. For those use cases, nothing else comes close.

**When ElevenLabs makes sense:** You're producing content, not consuming it. If you want the best possible AI voice for a project, ElevenLabs is it. If you want to listen to your reading list, it's not the right fit.

## 5. Browser Built-In TTS — Free, No Account, But Robotic

**Price:** Free

**Best for:** Occasional use when nothing else is available.

Every major browser has some form of built-in text-to-speech, and every operating system has its own TTS engine (macOS has Alex and Siri voices, Windows has Narrator, Android and iOS have built-in accessibility readers).

These are free, require no account, and work immediately. That's a real advantage.

The trade-offs are significant though. The voices are noticeably robotic compared to modern neural TTS. There's no word highlighting in most implementations. There's no document reader, no URL extraction, and no way to control playback in a meaningful way. It's functional for short bursts of listening, not for sustained reading sessions.

If you're in a pinch — you need to listen to something right now, you're on a locked-down computer, or you don't want to sign up for anything — built-in TTS does the job. For regular use, the experience degrades quickly.

## FAQ

### Is NaturalReader free?

NaturalReader has a free tier, but it's limited. The free version restricts which voices you can use and how much text you can convert in a session. As of 2026, NaturalReader also requires you to create an account before you can use the product at all — including the free tier. The paid plans start at $9.99/month ($99.50/year).

### What's a good free NaturalReader alternative?

Microsoft Immersive Reader is free and has word highlighting, but only works within Microsoft apps. Browser built-in TTS (using your OS accessibility features or browser extensions) is completely free and requires no account, but the voice quality is noticeably robotic. If you want natural voices for free, ReadTo offers a 60-second preview without creating an account — it's not unlimited free, but it lets you hear the quality before committing.

### Does any text-to-speech tool work without creating an account?

Yes. ReadTo lets you paste text and listen for up to 60 seconds without signing up — no email required. This is specifically designed so you can hear whether the voice quality is worth it before creating an account. Browser built-in TTS and OS accessibility readers also require no account at all, though voice quality is lower. Most other dedicated TTS tools (Speechify, NaturalReader, ElevenLabs) require account creation before use.

### What's the best NaturalReader alternative for dyslexia?

For dyslexia specifically, word-by-word highlighting is the most important feature — it removes the need to track where you are on the page while reading. ReadTo has this as a core feature. Speechify also has it. Microsoft Immersive Reader (free) has word highlighting and an OpenDyslexic font option, but only works in Microsoft apps. Ironically, NaturalReader's web version doesn't include word highlighting despite being one of the oldest accessibility-focused TTS tools.

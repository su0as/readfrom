## Three Ways to Listen to Articles Online

### Option 1: Browser Built-in Text-to-Speech

Every modern browser has some form of text-to-speech baked in. On a Mac, you can highlight text and right-click to have it read aloud. Microsoft Edge has a built-in "Read Aloud" feature in the toolbar. Safari supports it too.

**The upside:** It's already there. No setup required.

**The downside:** The voices are noticeably robotic. There's no word highlighting. You can't easily control speed. It reads everything — ads, navigation labels, cookie notices — not just the article. And it stops the moment you click anywhere else on the page.

It's fine if you need to listen to a single paragraph occasionally. It's not a real solution for clearing a backlog.

### Option 2: Browser Extensions

There's a class of Chrome and Firefox extensions built specifically to read web pages aloud. They sit in your toolbar, detect the main content on a page, and read it in a somewhat better voice than the browser default.

**The upside:** Works in the browser, easy to trigger, better than built-in TTS.

**The downside:** Voice quality is still limited by what the extension can access. Highlighting is inconsistent — some extensions support it, most don't. Many have rate limits on their free tiers. And you have to be on the actual article page — you can't queue up multiple links to listen to later.

For more options in this category, see our roundup of [Speechify alternatives](/blog/speechify-alternatives) — it covers both browser-based and app-based tools side by side.

This is where it gets genuinely useful. Tools like [ReadTo](https://www.readto.app) let you **paste a URL** and they extract just the readable content from the page — no ads, no nav, no junk — then convert it to speech with a natural AI voice.

You're not tied to the article page. You can queue it up, adjust speed, and listen while doing something else entirely. Word-by-word highlighting keeps you anchored if you glance at the screen. And because it's URL-to-audio, you can turn any article into audio in about five seconds.

This is the closest thing to "articles as podcasts" that currently exists. And it works on basically any public URL.
